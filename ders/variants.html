Variant readings in the Quran  

The Quran is well-known for its rich oral tradition and the diversity of its recitations, known as qira'at. The variant readings have been a subject of extensive study, reflecting both linguistic nuances and the depth of the Quranic text. The Quran is not a single text, but rather it is multi-faceted and contains numerous words with different readings. These differences arise primarily for two main reasons:

  • Phonetic variations: Different readings are permitted to accommodate variations in various dialects of Arabic, enriching the oral recitation of the Quran. For example, in Surah Ad-Duha, some readers pronounce the word as "ḍuḥā", while others read it as "ḍuḥæ" or "ḍuḥē".
  • Semantic richness: When differences in reading affect meaning, both interpretations are considered correct, adding depth to the Quranic message. For instance, in Surah al-Fatiha (1:4), the word is readable both as "malik" (king) and "mālik" (owner), conveying complementary aspects of the divine authority.

Close to 1200 words are marked in Iqra as having a variant reading. About 73% of these variants are related to the vowels, consonants do not change. In 320 words, consonants are allowed to change, and almost all are single-letter variations. 6 words may have a two-letter variation, and there are no variants with more than two letters.

The numbers clearly refute both of the following claims:

• Claim by some Muslims: "Variant readings are merely differences in regional pronunciation." This is incorrect. While phonetic variations constitue the majority, approximately 1,000 words (including repetitions) exhibit slight differences in meaning across various readings.

• Claim by some non-Muslims: "There are numerous instances of omitted and inserted words, even entire verses." In reality, within the valid readings, both verse order and word order remain fixed. The only cases of omission or addition involve a two-letter word (min) and the removal of another two-letter word (huwa), as noted in the list of two-letter variations above. There is no instance of verse order or word order being altered.


Classification and examples

Some orientalists have attempted to portray the variations as textual corruption. However, Islamic scholars assert that these readings were divinely revealed and that the Prophet Muhammad recited both at different times. It is inconceivable that the companions, who meticulously preserved the Quran, would have overlooked the difference between "Malik" and "Mâlik". Since both readings have been transmitted through mass transmission (tawātur), both meanings are equally valid and part of the Quranic revelation.

There are only a small number of acceptable recitations that go all the way back to the companions with a chain of narration. For a recitation to be valid, both verbal and written evidence is required: a solid chain of narration and conformity with one of the written mushafs. Anyone who claims "That word can also be read like this" is bound with the two rules.

Al-Dānī described the seven canonical readings in his book al-Taysīr. These readers (called qâri) are given below, along with the region they lived and the hijri year of death.

Variant readings can be classified into five groups:

  1. Pronunciation variants -- the same meaning
  2. Vowelization variants -- the same letters, 870 words
  3. Consonnantal variants -- the same root, 260 words
  4. Different root (yellow) -- the same spelling, 15 words
  5. Different spelling (pink) -- 45 words

1. Pronunciation variants

In most of the variant readings, the letters remain the same, only the pronunciation is different: the spelling and meaning of the word do not change. Since their number is quite large, only a small part of this group is displayed in the Iqra software.

Nāziʿāt 79:15 — Mūsā ⇒ Mūsæ | Mūsē In this verse, the word "Mūsā" readable in three different ways, depending on the local accent. There are many similar examples: "hudâ", "hawâ", "tajallâ" and similar words are written with the dotless "ya" instead of the alif, to allow for different readings. Similarly, there are qâris who read the words "nâr" and "kâfirûn" as "niyr" and "kiyfirûn"! At first hearing, they sound very different, but different accents really add richness to the sound.

2. Vowelization variants

In this category, the word remains the same, only the pronunciation is different.

Māidah 5:6 — arjulakum ⇒ arjulikum In this example, the meaning really gets richer: According to half of the readers, it is necessary to wash the feet, and according to the others, it is necessary to wipe the feet. There are interpretations that include both meanings. We know that the companions washed the feet most of the time and sometimes wiped the feet.

3. Consonnantal variants

Most of the consonant differences are related to verbs. Differences in letters or vowels have been recorded in matters such as the subject (mood) of the verb, whether it is active or passive, and which person it is.

Hijr 15:8 — nunazzilu ⇒ tanazzalu | tunazzalu While the readers of Kufa read this verse as meaning "we send down the angels", others say "the angels descend". The words are different but the meaning is the same.

4. Different root

In 14 verses, two words with different meanings have the same spelling. Both meanings are valid together and they enrich the meaning. Since one is not considered "more correct" than the other, both are preserved in memory, but are depicted in the same way in the Uthmani mushafs.

ʿAnkabūt 29:58 — nubawwiʾanna ⇒ nuṯwiyanna The two words sound very different, but without the dots, the spelling in Kufic script does not change. The meaning is also very close: To settle in a place is to make that place your home.

5. Different spelling

In 44 verses, the spelling of a word can change: one or two letters are different. In order to record the differences in reading, each mushaf was written according to the reading appropriate to the region it was going to. Since one was not considered "more correct" than the other, both were recorded as "regional differences" in the Uthmani mushafs. You can see all the spelling differences in the table given by Altıkulaç.

Yūnus 10:22 — yusayyirukum ⇒ yanšurukum Ibn ʿĀmir alone read this verse with a different word that is very similar in meaning. The root sin-ya-ra, which is the choice of the majority, means "to roam on the earth", while the root nun-šin-ra means "to spread". The difference is not easily seen in Kufic script.

Sources

Three centuries after the Hijra, in the 900s, an important classification was carried out in Baghdad, the center of the Abbasid Islamic state, regarding variant readings of the Quran: Ibn Mujahid compiled the readings of seven readers chosen from five regions (Medina, Mecca, Basra, Damascus, Kufa) and wrote his work called Kitāb al-Sabʿah fī al-Qirāʾāt (Seven Recitations). A century later, in Andalusia, Abu ʿAmr al-Dani simplified the seven readings in his famous book al-Taysīr.

In 1930, this work was republished in Istanbul by the German orientalist Otto Pretzl. About a century later, van Putten from Holland translated the same book into English and made it available for general use, adding some preliminary information. The tables found from the links on this page were extracted from van Putten's valuable work.
